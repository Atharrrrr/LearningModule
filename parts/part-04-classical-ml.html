Part IV · Chapter 4.1

Linear Regression

Chapter 1.3 fit a line through exam scores and called it "curve fitting." That technique has a real, official name — and this chapter extends it to combine several clues at once.

🏘️ Frame of reference

A real estate appraiser doesn't estimate a house's value from just one clue. They weigh square footage, number of bedrooms, and age together — each contributing its own share to the final number. Chapter 1.3's line only ever used one clue (hours studied). This chapter's whole upgrade is combining several clues into a single weighted prediction — otherwise, it's the exact same idea.

The technique already has a name

Linear regression is exactly Chapter 1.3's score = m × hours + b, officially named. With more than one feature, it becomes:

prediction = w₁×feature₁ + w₂×feature₂ + ... + bias

Weights: one knob per feature

Each feature gets its own weight — the general name for what Chapter 1.3 called m, now that there's more than one to keep track of. A larger-magnitude weight means that feature swings the prediction more; a weight near zero means it barely matters.

Worked example: pricing a house with two clues

Reusing Chapter 3.4's housing features, suppose price = 150 × sqft − 2000 × age + 50000. For a 2,000 sqft house that's 10 years old:

price = 150×2000 − 2000×10 + 50000 = 300,000 − 20,000 + 50,000 = $330,000

Part IV · Chapter 4.2

Logistic Regression

Despite the name, this isn't for predicting a number — it's for predicting a category, by squashing Chapter 4.1's weighted sum into a probability that can never leave 0 to 1.

🎚️ Frame of reference

Picture a confidence dial that physically cannot turn past its end stops — no matter how hard you push it, it curves and flattens out near 0% and 100% instead of shooting past them. Linear regression's raw weighted sum can output any number at all — even far below 0 or above 1, which makes no sense as a probability. Logistic regression bolts that confidence dial onto the end of the calculation, guaranteeing a sensible answer every time.

Squashing a number into a probability

Logistic regression is for classification — predicting a category, not a number. It computes the exact same weighted sum as Chapter 4.1's linear regression, then passes that raw score through the sigmoid function, which squashes any real number into a value between 0 and 1. That output is read as a probability: "92% chance this is spam." A threshold — commonly 0.5 — turns the probability into a final yes/no decision.

Worked example: scoring an email

Say a spam filter's weighted sum for one email comes out to 2.5 (positive score = spam-leaning). The sigmoid function turns that into sigmoid(2.5) ≈ 0.92 — a 92% chance of spam, comfortably over the 0.5 threshold, so it's classified spam. A different email scoring -1.5 gives sigmoid(-1.5) ≈ 0.18 — 18%, classified not spam.

Part IV · Chapter 4.3

How Learning Actually Happens

Remember dragging sliders in Chapter 1.3 to shrink that "average gap" number by hand? This chapter names what you were really doing, and hands the job to an algorithm that does it automatically — and far faster.

🌫️ Frame of reference

Imagine standing on a hilly landscape in thick fog. You can't see the whole terrain, but you can feel which direction is downhill right where you're standing. So you take a small step that way, feel the slope again, take another small step, and repeat — eventually reaching a low point, without ever seeing the full map. That's the entire idea. The "landscape" is how wrong a model's predictions are; "downhill" is making them less wrong; and "feeling the slope" is exactly Chapter 2.8's derivative.

Loss: the "average gap," formalized

Loss is Chapter 1.3's "average gap from the dots," formalized: a single number where bigger means worse. Different problems use different exact loss formulas, but the idea is always the same — measure how far off the predictions are, and try to shrink that number.

Gradient descent: walking downhill automatically

Gradient descent is the fog analogy, done with math instead of feet: compute the slope of the loss with respect to each parameter (Chapter 2.8's derivative), then nudge every parameter a small step in the downhill direction. Repeat, over and over, until the loss stops improving much. The learning rate controls how big each step is — Chapters 6.5 and 6.6 return to this in more depth.

Worked example: the exam-score line, automated

Chapter 1.3 started at m=1, b=10 (loss ≈ 54.9) and asked you to drag sliders toward m=6, b=45 (loss ≈ 1.3) by hand. Gradient descent finds that exact same destination on its own — watch it happen below.

Part IV · Chapter 4.4

k-Nearest Neighbors

No weights, no sigmoid, no gradient descent — just ask the closest examples what they think, and go with the majority.

🏡 Frame of reference

A real estate appraiser pricing a house doesn't run a formula from scratch — they pull up "comps": the handful of most similar recently-sold houses nearby, and base the estimate on what those actually sold for. No line-fitting, no training. Just find the closest matches and let them answer.

Let the neighbors vote

k-Nearest Neighbors finds the k closest points to a new example — using distance, a different notion of "similar" than Chapter 2.4's dot product — and lets them vote. Whichever label is most common among those k neighbors wins. There's no training step at all: the "model" is just the stored data, and all the work happens the moment a prediction is requested.

Choosing k matters

A small k (like 1) makes the prediction hinge on a single closest point — great if that point is representative, risky if it's a noisy outlier. A larger k smooths things out by averaging over more neighbors, but can blur real local patterns and drift toward whatever's most common overall.

Worked example: is the new movie a franchise film?

Reusing the movie vectors from Chapter 2.2, each now labeled Franchise or Not: a new movie at (7, 3) is closest to (8, 2) — distance √2 ≈ 1.41, a Franchise film — so k=1 predicts Franchise. Try different positions and k values below.

Part IV · Chapter 4.5

Decision Trees

Every other technique so far has predicted with arithmetic. This one predicts by asking questions — and you can read off its exact reasoning afterward.

❓ Frame of reference

20 Questions: narrow down an unknown answer by asking a sequence of yes/no questions, each one splitting the remaining possibilities in two, until only one answer is left. A decision tree does exactly this with data — ask a question about a feature, branch based on the answer, ask another question, until arriving at a final prediction.

Questions, branches, and a final answer

A decision tree asks one question about a feature at each step ("is square footage over 2,000?"), branches based on the answer, and repeats — until it reaches a leaf node: a final answer, no more questions. Building the tree means picking, at each step, the question that best separates the data into cleaner groups, then repeating on each resulting group.

Worked example: should I go for a run?

Part IV · Chapter 4.6

Random Forests & Boosting

A single decision tree is easy to read but easy to fool. Combine many of them, and the combination usually beats any one tree alone — in two very different ways.

🍬 Frame of reference

Ask one person to guess how many jellybeans are in a jar, and they might be way off. Ask fifty people independently and average their guesses, and the average is usually remarkably close to the true count — individual errors cancel out. That's the "wisdom of crowds," and it's exactly how a Random Forest works. Boosting is a different crowd strategy: instead of independent guessers, imagine a chain of students, each one specifically studying and fixing what the previous student got wrong.

Two ways to combine many trees

An ensemble combines many simple models into one stronger one. A random forest trains many decision trees independently, each on a random slice of the data and features, then averages or votes their predictions — independent guessers, wisdom of the crowd. Boosting instead trains trees sequentially: tree one predicts decently but misses a cluster of cases; tree two is trained specifically to fix that gap; tree three fixes whatever's still wrong after the first two. Add them together for the final prediction.

Worked example: fifty jellybean guesses

A jar actually holds 320 jellybeans. Five people guess: 280, 350, 300, 400, 310. Average = 1,640 ÷ 5 = 328 — far closer to 320 than several of the individual guesses were, even though nobody knew the real answer.

SVM & Naive Bayes

Two more classic classifiers, each built on a single sharp idea: one about geometry, one about Chapter 2.7's Bayes' theorem with a deliberate shortcut.

🚗 Frame of reference

Parking a car in an empty lot, you don't just avoid touching either line — you center the car for the widest possible margin on both sides. A Support Vector Machine picks its boundary the same way: not just any line that separates two classes, but the one leaving the most breathing room on both sides.

SVM: the widest possible margin

SVM finds the boundary with the widest possible margin between two classes. The points closest to that boundary — the support vectors — are the only ones that actually matter for defining it. Every other point could be moved, or deleted entirely, without the boundary shifting at all.

Naive Bayes: Chapter 2.7, with a shortcut

Naive Bayes applies Chapter 2.7's Bayes' theorem directly to classification — updating a prior into a posterior based on evidence — but adds one shortcut: it assumes every feature is independent of the others. An email containing "free" and an email containing "winner" are treated as two separate, unrelated clues, even though in reality spam emails that use one word are more likely to use the other too. That assumption is technically wrong — which is exactly why it's called "naive" — but the approximation is often good enough, and the computation is extremely fast, which is why Naive Bayes remains a popular, simple baseline for text classification like spam filtering.

  • Both are fast, classic, still-useful baseline techniques worth knowing even in a deep-learning-dominated field.

Part IV · Chapter 4.8

Clustering (K-means)

Every technique in this Part so far needed labels. This one finds groups in data with absolutely none — pure unsupervised learning (Chapter 1.2), in action.

🚚 Frame of reference

Imagine placing k food trucks in a large park with no data on where people will gather. You guess k starting spots, watch which truck each visitor ends up closest to, then move every truck to the center of the crowd it's currently serving. Repeat, and the trucks drift toward the park's natural gathering spots — without you ever telling them where those spots were.

Assign, then average, repeat

k-means picks k starting centroids, then alternates two steps: assign every point to its nearest centroid (using distance, Chapter 4.4's idea), then move each centroid to the average position of the points now assigned to it. Repeat until the centroids stop moving. No labels anywhere — just points and distance.

Part IV · Chapter 4.9

Dimensionality Reduction (PCA/t-SNE)

Real datasets often have dozens or hundreds of features — far too many to plot (Chapter 2.12). This chapter compresses them down to a handful, without throwing away what matters most.

🔦 Frame of reference

Hold a wire sculpture up to a light and it casts a 2D shadow on the wall. Rotate it, and some angles produce a shadow that barely looks like anything — a thin sliver. Other angles cast a shadow that captures the sculpture's real shape surprisingly well. Finding that best angle — the one preserving the most real information in fewer dimensions — is exactly what dimensionality reduction does with data.

PCA: finding the best angle automatically

Dimensionality reduction compresses high-dimensional data into fewer dimensions while keeping as much important structure as possible. PCA does this by finding the direction with the greatest spread in the data and projecting every point onto it — the mathematical version of finding the best shadow angle. You'll search for that angle by hand below; PCA finds it automatically, the same way gradient descent (4.3) automated manual line-fitting.

t-SNE: built for visualization

t-SNE takes a different approach. Instead of preserving overall spread like PCA, it prioritizes keeping nearby points nearby — points that were close together in high-dimensional space stay close together in the 2D result, even if that distorts the bigger picture. This tends to produce visually clean clusters, which is why t-SNE is a favorite for plotting things like word embeddings (Chapter 8.3) in two dimensions for humans to actually look at.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Dimensionality reduction compresses high-dimensional data into fewer dimensions while preserving important structure.
  • PCA finds the direction(s) of greatest spread and projects points onto them — like finding the best shadow angle.
  • t-SNE is nonlinear and prioritizes keeping nearby points nearby, favored for visualization.
  • Both make it possible to plot and understand data that would otherwise have too many dimensions to visualize directly.
