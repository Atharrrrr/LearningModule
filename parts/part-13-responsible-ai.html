Fairness & Bias

Chapter 3.7 already established that biased data produces biased models. This chapter makes that concrete and measurable: a model can post a perfectly respectable overall accuracy (Chapter 5.3) while being systematically worse for one group of people than another — and overall accuracy alone will never reveal that, because it averages right over the disparity.

⚖️ Frame of reference

Imagine a loan officer trained purely by studying decades of past lending decisions — decisions that themselves reflected real historical discrimination. The officer never explicitly considers anything they shouldn't, and yet, having learned exclusively from that historical record, they end up confidently reproducing its same old patterns, believing they're just reading the data objectively. A model trained the same way inherits the exact same risk — and unlike a human, it applies whatever pattern it learned with total consistency, at whatever scale it's deployed.

Overall accuracy can hide a real disparity

Chapter 3.7's bias (in data and models) is the root cause; this chapter is about detecting its effect once a model exists. A single accuracy number (Chapter 5.3) is computed across an entire dataset — it has no way to reveal that a model performs very differently for one subgroup than another, because good performance on one group can average out with poor performance on another to look perfectly fine overall.

Measuring fairness directly, per group

Two concrete, computable checks catch different kinds of disparity. Demographic parity compares how often each group gets a positive prediction at all. Equal opportunity compares recall (Chapter 5.3) across groups — specifically among people who are genuinely positive, how many does the model actually catch in each group? A model can pass one of these checks while failing the other badly; real fairness work usually has to pick which disparity matters most for the specific decision at hand, since satisfying every possible fairness definition simultaneously is, in general, mathematically impossible once the underlying groups have different base rates.

Overall accuracy comes out to a respectable 80% — nothing about that single number raises any flag. But look at recall per group: 90% of genuinely qualified Group A applicants get approved, versus only 60% of equally qualified Group B applicants. That 30-point gap was there the entire time, completely hidden behind one aggregate accuracy figure. Fairness metrics exist specifically because "the overall number looks fine" and "no group is being systematically underserved" are two different claims, and only checking the first one can miss the second entirely.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Bias in training data (Chapter 3.7) produces a model that faithfully reproduces — and can amplify — whatever disparities were already present in that data.
  • A single overall accuracy figure can look completely fine while hiding a large performance gap between subgroups.
  • Demographic parity compares positive-prediction rates across groups; equal opportunity compares recall across groups — two different, concrete, computable fairness checks.
  • A model can satisfy one fairness definition while failing another; picking which disparity matters most is a real, unavoidable part of building responsible models.
Quick check
1. Why can a model's overall accuracy look completely fine while it's actually treating two groups very differently?
Overall accuracy averages performance across the entire dataset, so good performance on one group can offset poor performance on another without the aggregate number revealing the gap.
Overall accuracy is simply calculated incorrectly whenever subgroups exist.
Subgroup disparities never actually happen in real trained models.
2. What specifically does equal opportunity check, that demographic parity doesn't?
Whether people who are genuinely positive get correctly identified at the same rate across groups (recall), rather than just how often each group receives a positive prediction overall.
Nothing — demographic parity and equal opportunity are actually identical checks.
How the model will perform on entirely new data it hasn't been trained on yet.
3. Why can't a model always satisfy every possible fairness definition at the same time?
When groups have different underlying base rates, it's mathematically impossible in general to satisfy every fairness definition simultaneously — some tradeoff has to be made.
It's simply a matter of not having tried hard enough to build a better model.
Demographic parity and equal opportunity always agree with each other, so there's no real tradeoff to make.
