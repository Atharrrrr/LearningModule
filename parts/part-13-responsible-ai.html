Fairness & Bias

Chapter 3.7 already established that biased data produces biased models. This chapter makes that concrete and measurable: a model can post a perfectly respectable overall accuracy (Chapter 5.3) while being systematically worse for one group of people than another — and overall accuracy alone will never reveal that, because it averages right over the disparity.

⚖️ Frame of reference

Imagine a loan officer trained purely by studying decades of past lending decisions — decisions that themselves reflected real historical discrimination. The officer never explicitly considers anything they shouldn't, and yet, having learned exclusively from that historical record, they end up confidently reproducing its same old patterns, believing they're just reading the data objectively. A model trained the same way inherits the exact same risk — and unlike a human, it applies whatever pattern it learned with total consistency, at whatever scale it's deployed.

Overall accuracy can hide a real disparity

Chapter 3.7's bias (in data and models) is the root cause; this chapter is about detecting its effect once a model exists. A single accuracy number (Chapter 5.3) is computed across an entire dataset — it has no way to reveal that a model performs very differently for one subgroup than another, because good performance on one group can average out with poor performance on another to look perfectly fine overall.

Measuring fairness directly, per group

Two concrete, computable checks catch different kinds of disparity. Demographic parity compares how often each group gets a positive prediction at all. Equal opportunity compares recall (Chapter 5.3) across groups — specifically among people who are genuinely positive, how many does the model actually catch in each group? A model can pass one of these checks while failing the other badly; real fairness work usually has to pick which disparity matters most for the specific decision at hand, since satisfying every possible fairness definition simultaneously is, in general, mathematically impossible once the underlying groups have different base rates.

Part XIII · Chapter 13.2

Privacy, Safety & Misuse

Chapter 13.1 covered how a model can treat different groups of people unevenly. This chapter covers three more ways a trained model can cause real harm: leaking pieces of its own training data, being tricked into producing content it was trained to refuse, and being deliberately misused.

📓 Frame of reference

A student who reads a textbook doesn't memorize most of it word for word — they absorb the general ideas. But one strange, one-off sentence that appeared exactly once, with nothing similar anywhere else to generalize from, might stick in their memory verbatim, purely because there was no broader pattern to compress it into. Ask them to recall a common topic and you'll get a paraphrase; ask about that one weird sentence and you might get it back word for word. A trained model can do the exact same thing with its training data.

Privacy: models can memorize, not just generalize

Memorization is a real, measurable risk for models trained on huge amounts of real-world text (Chapter 9.2) — a person's phone number, address, or a private passage that happened to appear in scraped training data doesn't get "cleaned up" into a general rule the way a common phrase does; it can simply get stored close to verbatim. The right prompt can sometimes extract it back out, which is exactly why training data curation and filtering matter as much as raw scale.

● Interactive demo — try it

Chapter 9.2's exact toy next-word model, trained on a tiny corpus containing one common pattern (repeated with variety) and one rare, unique string (appearing only once). Compare what the model does with each.

Query: "cat" (a common pattern)
Query: "555" (a rare, unique string)

"Cat" splits its probability across two real, different continuations — a genuinely generalized pattern, learned from multiple training sentences. "555" is 100% deterministic — it appeared in the training data exactly once, so the model has no alternative to draw on and reproduces the specific string that followed it verbatim. That's the entire mechanism behind training-data memorization, honestly reproduced at toy scale: it isn't a bug, it's the direct, unavoidable consequence of a rare string having no repeated pattern to be compressed into.

Safety: models can be tricked into unwanted behavior

RLHF (Chapter 9.4) trains a model to refuse harmful requests, but that training only covers the patterns it was actually trained against. A jailbreak (adversarial prompt) is a request phrased specifically to slip past that trained behavior — through role-play framing, unusual formatting, or splitting a harmful request across several seemingly-innocent steps. Safety training genuinely reduces harmful output; it doesn't make a model unconditionally safe against every possible way of asking. This is an ongoing arms race between safety training and adversarial prompting, not a problem with a final, permanent fix.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Memorization happens when a rare or unique training example has no repeated pattern to be generalized into, so a model can reproduce it nearly verbatim instead of paraphrasing it.
  • Common patterns, seen many times with variation, get compressed into a genuinely generalized, spread-out probability distribution instead.
  • A jailbreak is a prompt specifically crafted to bypass a model's trained safety behavior (RLHF, Chapter 9.4).
  • Safety training reduces harmful output but doesn't eliminate it — it's an ongoing arms race, not a solved, permanent guarantee.
