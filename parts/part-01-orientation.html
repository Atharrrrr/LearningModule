Part I · Chapter 1.1

AI vs. ML vs. Deep Learning vs. RL

Four terms that get used almost interchangeably in headlines, but mean four different — and nested — things. Here's how they fit together.

🚗 Frame of reference

Imagine four different ways a car could be "smart" behind the wheel. One: a GPS follows a rule a programmer wrote once — "if the next turn is in 200 feet, announce it." Two: a system watches thousands of hours of real driving and learns, from that data, what good driving looks like. Three: a system learns directly from raw camera footage, automatically figuring out on its own which pixels mean "pedestrian" or "stop sign," without a human ever pointing those features out. Four: a system just drives — badly at first — and gets better only because it's penalized every time it drifts out of its lane and rewarded every time it doesn't, with no examples of "correct" driving shown to it at all. All four are trying to be smart. Only some of them are learning. And they nest inside each other, which is what this chapter is really about.

The outermost circle: AI

Artificial intelligence is the biggest, oldest, and vaguest of the four terms. It just means "getting a computer to do something that looks intelligent." That GPS following a rule someone typed in by hand? Still AI — it's old-school, rule-based AI, but it counts. AI is the goal; the other three terms below are all strategies for reaching it.

A strategy for AI: ML

Machine learning is a specific way of getting to AI: instead of a programmer writing out every rule by hand, the system is shown data — examples — and figures out its own rules from patterns in that data. The spam filter that learns which emails you flag is doing ML. The rule-based GPS from the analogy is not; it never looks at data to improve itself. Every ML system is AI, but not every AI system is ML.

A specific kind of ML: deep learning

Most modern ML systems are built out of a neural network. When that network is stacked into many layers — deep, layer upon layer — the field calls it deep learning. The payoff of "deep" is that the system can work from raw, messy input (a photo's raw pixels, say) and discover on its own which patterns matter, layer by layer, rather than a human deciding in advance that "edges matter" or "round shapes matter." Deep learning is a subset of ML, which is a subset of AI.

A different axis entirely: RL

Reinforcement learning is also a subset of ML, but it answers a different question than deep learning does. Deep learning is about how a system finds patterns (many layers, automatically). Reinforcement learning is about what it's learning from: not a pile of labeled examples, but its own trial and error, steered by reward and penalty. The two aren't opposites — an RL system can absolutely use a deep neural network internally. When it does, people call it "deep RL," and you'll meet it properly in Part XII.

Worked example: sort four real systems into their circle

Here's the test — for each system below, is it AI? Is it ML? Deep learning? RL?

  1. A thermostat that turns on the AC whenever a sensor reads above 78°F. A programmer wrote that rule once, and it never changes. → AI, but not ML — no data ever adjusted the rule.
  2. A spam filter that studies thousands of emails you've marked "spam" and learns which words and patterns predict it, updating as you flag more. → AI and ML — it improves from data.
  3. A dog-breed app that learns directly from millions of raw photos, automatically discovering which pixel patterns distinguish a beagle from a bulldog through many stacked layers. → AI, ML, and deep learning — a many-layered neural network finding its own features.
  4. A robot vacuum that bumps a wall, takes a small penalty, and gradually learns a cleaner path purely through trial and reward, with no example paths ever shown to it. → AI, ML, and reinforcement learning — learning from consequences, not labeled examples.

Part I · Chapter 1.2

The Three Ways Machines Learn

Chapter 1.1 nested AI, ML, deep learning, and RL inside each other. This chapter looks at ML from a different angle: not what it's built from, but what kind of feedback it learns from. There are exactly three answers.

🐦 Frame of reference

Imagine three different ways to learn to identify birds. With a guide, an expert walks beside you and names every bird the moment you both see it — "that's a robin," "that's a sparrow" — until, after enough examples, you can name them yourself. Alone with no guide at all, you just notice patterns on your own: "these grey ones always travel in flocks," "that red-chested one is usually alone" — you never learn their official names, but you learn to tell them apart. With a friend who only says "yes" or "no" after each guess you make out loud, you're never told a bird's name either — you just get better through repeated right-or-wrong feedback. Same skill, three completely different ways of learning it.

Way one: learning from an answer key

In supervised learning, every piece of training data comes with a label attached — the correct answer, decided in advance, usually by a human. An email is marked "spam" or "not spam." A photo is marked "cat" or "dog." The model's entire job is to guess the label, check itself against the real one, and nudge itself toward being right more often. This is the "guide who names every bird" from the analogy — it's learning from an answer key.

Way two: learning with no answer key at all

In unsupervised learning, there's no answer key — the data has no labels at all. The model can't check its guesses against anything, so instead it looks for structure: which examples are similar to each other, which are different, and how they naturally cluster into groups. It might discover that a set of emails splits cleanly into two groups without ever being told either group is "spam." This is the "no guide at all" version — patterns found entirely on your own.

Way three: learning from reward and penalty

The third way is reinforcement learning, already introduced in Chapter 1.1 — no labels and no answer key, just an action followed by a reward or a penalty. It's the "friend who only says yes or no" version: you're never shown what the right answer was, only whether what you just did worked out.

Worked example: one task, three ways

Take the spam filter from the last chapter and build it three different ways:

  1. Supervised: collect a pile of emails, each one hand-labeled "spam" or "not spam." Train a model to predict that label from the email's text.
  2. Unsupervised: collect the same pile of emails, but with no labels at all. The model finds that they naturally split into two clusters — one full of urgent-sounding, link-heavy messages, the other not — without ever using the word "spam."
  3. Reinforcement: the filter guesses spam or not spam for each incoming email, and gets a tiny reward when you leave its guess alone, and a penalty when you correct it. Over thousands of emails, it improves — without ever seeing a labeled training set up front.

Machines Don't Think, They Fit Curves

"The model learned that..." makes it sound like something is understanding. Underneath, something much simpler — and much more mechanical — is happening.

📏 Frame of reference

Tailors and drafters use a tool called a French curve — or sometimes just a bendy strip of flexible plastic — to draw a smooth line through a set of pinned points on paper. You don't calculate the curve; you just bend the strip until it passes as close as possible to every pin, then trace it. The strip doesn't understand the shape it's making. It's just bending until it fits. That bending-until-it-fits process — done with numbers instead of plastic — is, mechanically, most of what "a machine learned something" actually means.

What a "model" actually is

A model is just a function — take some input, do some math to it, get an output. What makes it a model rather than a fixed formula is that the math has knobs on it: adjustable numbers called parameters. Change the knobs and the exact same function produces different outputs for the same input. A very simple model might be as plain as score = m × hours + b — a straight line, where m and b are the two parameters, the knobs you can turn.

What "training" and "prediction" actually mean

Training is nothing more than searching for the knob settings — the parameter values — that make the model's curve pass as close as possible to a pile of real examples. Once those knobs are set, running the model on a brand new input — one it's never seen — to get an output is called making a prediction. The model never had to understand what "hours studied" or "exam score" mean as concepts. It only ever had to find numbers that made a curve match a pattern.

Worked example: fit a line to real-ish data

Say you have seven students, and for each one you know hours studied and their exam score. Try two different knob settings for score = m × hours + b:

  • m = 1, b = 10 — for a student who studied 7 hours, this predicts a score of 17. Every real student in the data scored way higher than that. A bad fit — the line is too flat and starts too low.
  • m = 6, b = 45 — for that same 7-hour student, this predicts 87. The real students' scores land close to this line all the way across. A good fit.
