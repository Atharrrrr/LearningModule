Why Models Fail (Over/Underfitting, Bias-Variance)

A model can fail in exactly two directions: too simple, or too clever for its own good. Both look fine on paper until you check the wrong number.

🎯 Frame of reference

Picture four archery targets. Target one: every arrow lands in a tight cluster, but far from the bullseye — consistently wrong in the same way. Target two: arrows scattered all over, some close, some far — inconsistent, but averaging out near the center. Target three: a tight cluster right on the bullseye — the goal. Target four: scattered and off-center — the worst case. Those first two targets are exactly the two ways a model fails.

Too simple, or too clever

Underfitting is target one: the model is too simple to capture the real pattern, so it does poorly on training data and new data alike — high bias. Overfitting is target two: the model is complex enough to memorize the training data's every quirk, including its noise, so it looks great on training data but falls apart on anything new — high variance. Note: this "bias" is a different concept from Chapter 3.7's data/model fairness bias — same word, unrelated meaning.

The bias-variance tradeoff

The bias-variance tradeoff is the tension between these two failure modes: making a model more flexible reduces bias but raises variance, and vice versa. The goal is the sweet spot in between — capturing the real pattern without memorizing the noise.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Underfitting: a model too simple to capture the real pattern — high error everywhere. High bias.
  • Overfitting: a model too complex, memorizing training data's noise — near-zero training error, poor performance on new data. High variance.
  • The bias-variance tradeoff: reducing one often increases the other; the goal is the sweet spot between them.
  • This chapter's "bias" is a different concept from Chapter 3.7's data/model fairness bias.
Quick check
1. A model has very low error on training data but performs poorly on new data it hasn't seen. What's happening?
Overfitting — the model memorized the training data, including its noise.
Underfitting — the model is too simple.
The model is working exactly as intended; this is normal and desirable.
2. In the archery-target analogy, what does "high bias, low variance" look like?
A tight cluster of arrows, consistently landing far from the bullseye.
Arrows scattered all over the target, on average near the bullseye.
A single perfect bullseye hit, every time.
