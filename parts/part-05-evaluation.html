Part V · Chapter 5.1

Why Models Fail (Over/Underfitting, Bias-Variance)

A model can fail in exactly two directions: too simple, or too clever for its own good. Both look fine on paper until you check the wrong number.

🎯 Frame of reference

Picture four archery targets. Target one: every arrow lands in a tight cluster, but far from the bullseye — consistently wrong in the same way. Target two: arrows scattered all over, some close, some far — inconsistent, but averaging out near the center. Target three: a tight cluster right on the bullseye — the goal. Target four: scattered and off-center — the worst case. Those first two targets are exactly the two ways a model fails.

Too simple, or too clever

Underfitting is target one: the model is too simple to capture the real pattern, so it does poorly on training data and new data alike — high bias. Overfitting is target two: the model is complex enough to memorize the training data's every quirk, including its noise, so it looks great on training data but falls apart on anything new — high variance. Note: this "bias" is a different concept from Chapter 3.7's data/model fairness bias — same word, unrelated meaning.

The bias-variance tradeoff

The bias-variance tradeoff is the tension between these two failure modes: making a model more flexible reduces bias but raises variance, and vice versa. The goal is the sweet spot in between — capturing the real pattern without memorizing the noise.

Part V · Chapter 5.2

Train/Val/Test & Cross-Validation

Chapter 5.1 showed that low training error can be a trap. The only honest fix is evaluating a model on data it has never seen — and doing that requires real discipline about which data gets used for what.

📝 Frame of reference

Studying for an exam: the practice problems are what you learn from. A practice test, taken before the real thing, checks your understanding and helps you adjust your studying — but seeing it too many times just teaches you those specific questions. The final exam, taken exactly once, is what actually determines your grade. Peeking at the final exam early would make your score meaningless. A model's data needs the exact same three roles.

Three sets, three jobs

The train/validation/test split mirrors the exam analogy exactly: the training set is the practice problems the model actually learns from. The validation set is the practice test — used repeatedly during development to tune choices and catch overfitting (Chapter 5.1), but never used to fit parameters directly. The test set is the final exam: touched exactly once, at the very end, for an honest read on how the model will really perform.

Cross-validation: several practice tests instead of one

A single validation split might, by luck, cover exactly the material you know best — or worst. Cross-validation fixes this by splitting the training data into k folds, training k separate times, each time holding out a different fold as validation, then averaging all k results — several practice tests instead of relying on just one.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • A training set fits the model's parameters; a validation set tunes choices during development; a test set is touched only once, for an honest final estimate.
  • Never let test-set performance influence development decisions — that quietly inflates apparent results.
  • Cross-validation splits training data into k folds, rotating which fold is validation, training k times and averaging.
  • This is the practical machinery behind catching Chapter 5.1's overfitting before it's too late.
