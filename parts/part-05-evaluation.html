Part V · Chapter 5.1

Why Models Fail (Over/Underfitting, Bias-Variance)

A model can fail in exactly two directions: too simple, or too clever for its own good. Both look fine on paper until you check the wrong number.

🎯 Frame of reference

Picture four archery targets. Target one: every arrow lands in a tight cluster, but far from the bullseye — consistently wrong in the same way. Target two: arrows scattered all over, some close, some far — inconsistent, but averaging out near the center. Target three: a tight cluster right on the bullseye — the goal. Target four: scattered and off-center — the worst case. Those first two targets are exactly the two ways a model fails.

Too simple, or too clever

Underfitting is target one: the model is too simple to capture the real pattern, so it does poorly on training data and new data alike — high bias. Overfitting is target two: the model is complex enough to memorize the training data's every quirk, including its noise, so it looks great on training data but falls apart on anything new — high variance. Note: this "bias" is a different concept from Chapter 3.7's data/model fairness bias — same word, unrelated meaning.

The bias-variance tradeoff

The bias-variance tradeoff is the tension between these two failure modes: making a model more flexible reduces bias but raises variance, and vice versa. The goal is the sweet spot in between — capturing the real pattern without memorizing the noise.

Part V · Chapter 5.2

Train/Val/Test & Cross-Validation

Chapter 5.1 showed that low training error can be a trap. The only honest fix is evaluating a model on data it has never seen — and doing that requires real discipline about which data gets used for what.

📝 Frame of reference

Studying for an exam: the practice problems are what you learn from. A practice test, taken before the real thing, checks your understanding and helps you adjust your studying — but seeing it too many times just teaches you those specific questions. The final exam, taken exactly once, is what actually determines your grade. Peeking at the final exam early would make your score meaningless. A model's data needs the exact same three roles.

Three sets, three jobs

The train/validation/test split mirrors the exam analogy exactly: the training set is the practice problems the model actually learns from. The validation set is the practice test — used repeatedly during development to tune choices and catch overfitting (Chapter 5.1), but never used to fit parameters directly. The test set is the final exam: touched exactly once, at the very end, for an honest read on how the model will really perform.

Cross-validation: several practice tests instead of one

A single validation split might, by luck, cover exactly the material you know best — or worst. Cross-validation fixes this by splitting the training data into k folds, training k separate times, each time holding out a different fold as validation, then averaging all k results — several practice tests instead of relying on just one.

Part V · Chapter 5.3

Metrics (Precision, Recall, F1, ROC-AUC)

Chapter 3.5 showed accuracy can lie: a model that always says "not fraud" scores 99% while catching zero fraud. This chapter gives you the numbers that don't lie — and the machinery to compute them.

🎣 Frame of reference

Casting a net into a lake that holds fish, seaweed, and driftwood. Precision asks: of everything the net pulled up, how much was actually fish? Recall asks: of every fish actually in the lake, how many did the net catch? A net with huge holes catches nearly every fish (great recall) but also hauls up junk (poor precision). A net with a tiny opening catches only what swims right into it (poor recall) but almost nothing else (great precision). Neither number alone tells the whole story.

When accuracy lies: precision and recall

Precision and recall are the fishing-net questions turned into arithmetic, using the same true-positive (TP), false-positive (FP), false-negative (FN), and true-negative (TN) counts from Chapter 2.7's Bayes' theorem story and Chapter 3.5's fraud grid:

  • Precision = TP / (TP + FP) — of the alarms raised, how many were real?
  • Recall = TP / (TP + FN) — of the real cases, how many were caught?

Worked example: recomputing Chapter 3.5's fraud detector

Chapter 3.5's "decent" model, scaled to 1,000 transactions (10 fraud, 990 legitimate), caught 9 of the 10 fraud cases but flagged 39 legitimate transactions by mistake — landing at 96% accuracy. In TP/FP/FN/TN terms: TP = 9, FN = 1, FP = 39, TN = 951.

  • Precision = 9 / (9 + 39) = 9 / 48 ≈ 19% — most alarms were false alarms.
  • Recall = 9 / (9 + 1) = 90% — it caught almost all the real fraud.

96% accuracy sounded great. 19% precision tells the real story: four out of every five fraud alerts this model raises are wrong. Whether that trade-off is acceptable depends entirely on what a false alarm costs versus what missed fraud costs — a business decision, not a math one.

One number that balances both: F1

Optimizing for recall alone is trivial — flag everything, and recall hits 100% (this is exactly the failure mode a huge-holed net has). Optimizing for precision alone is also trivial — flag only the single most obvious case. The F1 score punishes both extremes by taking the harmonic mean rather than the plain average, which stays low unless precision and recall are both reasonably high.

For the fraud example above: F1 = 2 × (0.19 × 0.90) / (0.19 + 0.90) = 0.342 / 1.09 ≈ 31% — a single number reflecting that recall was strong but precision was weak, pulling the combined score down toward the lower of the two.

ROC-AUC: one number across every threshold

Precision and recall both depend on one specific decision threshold — the cutoff score above which the model shouts "fraud!" Move the threshold and both numbers move too. ROC-AUC sidesteps that by asking a threshold-free question: across every possible cutoff, how well does the model's score rank real positives above real negatives?

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Precision = TP/(TP+FP): of the positive predictions, how many were right. Recall = TP/(TP+FN): of the real positives, how many were caught.
  • Neither number alone is the full picture — a model can trivially max out either one at the other's expense.
  • F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, staying low unless both are reasonably high.
  • ROC-AUC summarizes a model's ranking ability across every decision threshold at once, in a single number from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect).

The harmonic mean punishes a big gap between the two, whereas a plain average could hide one very weak number behind one very strong one.

Part V · Chapter 5.4

Regularization

Chapter 5.1 showed overfitting happens when a model gets too clever — memorizing noise instead of learning the real pattern. Regularization is the training-time fix: a built-in penalty for cleverness that isn't earning its keep.

🪒 Frame of reference

Occam's razor: when two explanations fit the same observations equally well, prefer the simpler one — extra complexity that doesn't buy extra explanatory power is more likely to be chasing noise than capturing something real. Regularization is Occam's razor written directly into a training algorithm: it penalizes unnecessary complexity (large weights) unless the data truly justifies it.

A penalty for weights that aren't earning their keep

Chapter 4.1 built a house-price model from real features: weight-carrying inputs like square footage and age. But with enough training data, almost any feature — even a meaningless one — shows some tiny spurious correlation with the target by pure chance, and an unregularized model will happily assign it a nonzero weight anyway. Regularization fixes this by penalizing large weights during training, so a feature only keeps a sizable weight if it's actually pulling its weight.

The two standard penalty shapes are L1/L2 regularization. L1 is the more dramatic of the two — it can zero out a weight entirely, effectively removing that feature from the model. L2 is gentler, shrinking every weight toward zero without usually eliminating any outright. The demo below uses an L1-style rule (subtract a fixed penalty, floor at zero — a real technique called soft-thresholding) so you can watch weights actually hit zero.

Worked example: a red herring in the housing model

Suppose four features go into training a house-price model: square footage, number of bedrooms, house age, and — thrown in as a red herring — the number of vowels in the street name. Before any regularization, imagine training assigned these raw importances: sqft = 85, bedrooms = 60, age = 45, vowels = 12. Vowels-in-street-name is obvious nonsense, but it still has some nonzero importance, purely from noise.

● Interactive demo — try it

Increase the regularization strength (λ) and watch each weight shrink toward zero — the least useful feature first.

Notice the order: the irrelevant feature (vowels) hits zero first, since it started with the least real signal to defend. Push λ far enough and even genuinely useful features — square footage, the strongest real signal — get zeroed out too. That's not a bug; it's the same over/underfitting tradeoff from Chapter 5.1, now with a dial. Too little regularization invites overfitting; too much causes underfitting. Choosing λ is itself a job for the validation set from Chapter 5.2.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Regularization adds a penalty for large weights during training, discouraging the model from leaning too heavily on any one feature.
  • L1 regularization can zero out unhelpful weights entirely; L2 shrinks every weight somewhat without usually eliminating any.
  • Irrelevant features, carrying little real signal, tend to get zeroed out first as regularization strength increases.
  • The regularization strength is a dial on the bias-variance tradeoff (Chapter 5.1): too little risks overfitting, too much causes underfitting.
