The Neuron

You already built one of these back in Chapter 4.2 — you just didn't call it by its real name. This chapter gives it a name, a diagram, and the vocabulary the rest of Part VI builds on.

🚪 Frame of reference

A bouncer deciding whether to let someone into a club. They weigh several pieces of evidence — is the ID clearly real? Are they on the guest list? Are they dressed for the venue? — and each piece of evidence matters a different amount. The bouncer adds up the weighted evidence in their head, compares it to their own personal threshold for "convincing enough," and decides: let them in, or not.

The same weighted sum, with a new name

A neuron takes several inputs, multiplies each one by its own weight (Chapter 4.1), adds them all up, and adds one more adjustable number called the bias term — not to be confused with either the data/model fairness "bias" from Chapter 3.7 or the statistical "bias" from Chapter 5.1's bias-variance tradeoff. A neuron's bias term is just an offset, the same role the "+50000" played in Chapter 4.1's housing formula. That combined number then passes through an activation function (Chapter 6.3 covers the options) to produce the neuron's output.

If that sounds familiar, it should: Chapter 4.2's logistic regression — weighted sum, plus bias, through the sigmoid function — is a single neuron. Logistic regression was a neuron all along. What's new in this Part is connecting many of them together.

● Interactive demo — try it

Should you bring an umbrella? Adjust the weights and bias and watch the neuron decide, live.

Notice the shape of the computation never changes as you drag sliders — only the numbers do. That's the whole idea of a neuron: a fixed, simple recipe (weighted sum, add bias, squash through an activation function) whose behavior is entirely controlled by its weights and bias. Training a neural network (Chapter 6.4 onward) means searching for good values for exactly those numbers — nothing more exotic than that.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • A neuron multiplies each input by its own weight, sums the results, adds a bias term, then passes the total through an activation function.
  • Chapter 4.2's logistic regression is exactly one neuron using the sigmoid activation function.
  • A neuron's "bias term" is an adjustable offset — a different meaning of "bias" than data/model fairness (Chapter 3.7) or the statistical bias-variance tradeoff (Chapter 5.1).
  • All of a neuron's behavior lives in its weights and bias; training means searching for good values for them.
