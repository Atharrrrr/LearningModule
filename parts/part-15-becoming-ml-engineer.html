Part XV · Chapter 15.1

How to Read a Research Paper

Every "Meet X" chapter in this book (Chapter 7.7's YOLO, 7.8's SuperPoint, 8.6's SuperGlue) has been a summary of a real research paper, written to be readable with zero prerequisites. The real papers behind them are not written that way — they're written by researchers, for other researchers, under real length limits. This chapter is about closing that gap: a concrete strategy for reading one directly, using a paper this book has already summarized for you as the worked example, so you can check every claim yourself.

🪑 Frame of reference

Nobody assembles flat-pack furniture by reading the instruction booklet word one to word last, front to back. They look at the finished-product photo on the box first — what am I even building? Then the parts diagram — what pieces do I have to work with? Only then do they open the numbered text instructions, and usually only closely for the one step where they're actually stuck. A research paper rewards exactly that same out-of-order, purpose-driven reading — not a novel, read start to finish.

A paper's anatomy is predictable — use that

Nearly every ML research paper follows the same skeleton: Title, abstract, Introduction, Related Work, Method, Experiments, Conclusion, References. That predictability is the whole advantage — once you know what each section is for, you can read strategically instead of linearly: title and abstract first (what is this?), then the figures and conclusion (what did they find?), then the introduction (why does it matter?), and only then the method in full, slowly, often twice (how does it actually work?) — with related work and the fine print of experiments read on demand, not by default.

Reading Experiments sections: know what an ablation study is

One recurring pattern worth recognizing on sight: an ablation study. If SuperPoint's paper reports results with and without homographic adaptation, or with a shared backbone versus two separate networks, that's an ablation — evidence for which specific design choice mattered, not just a claim that the final system works. A results table with only one row for "our method" is weaker evidence than one showing what happens when a specific piece is removed.

  • The real SuperPoint paper's Method section maps directly onto Chapter 7.8's base detector, homographic adaptation, and shared-backbone-plus-two-heads architecture — this book's summaries are not a substitute for the real thing, but a way in.

Choosing a Specialization

Chapter 1.5 covered one axis of "which AI job" — Research Scientist, ML Engineer, MLOps, or Data Scientist, a question about which function you'd do. Having now built something from nearly every Part of this book, a second, independent axis opens up: which domain. A Research Scientist working on generative image models and a Research Scientist working on LLM alignment do genuinely different work, day to day, despite sharing a job title. This chapter is about that second axis.

🔪 Frame of reference

Culinary school teaches every working chef the same foundation — knife skills, heat control, sauces, food safety — regardless of what they'll cook for a living. Nobody graduates and then tries to be simultaneously world-class at pastry, sushi, butchery, and fine dining all at once. A working chef keeps the general foundation everywhere, but goes deliberately deep in one station. This book just gave you the culinary school. This chapter is about picking a station.

Depth in one area, a real foundation everywhere else

What this book has built, chapter by chapter, is the horizontal bar of a T-shaped skill set — a real, working foundation across classical ML, neural networks, vision, language, multimodal systems, generative models, RL, and deployment. Almost nobody gets hired to be equally expert at all of it simultaneously; real teams hire for depth in one vertical stroke, resting on exactly that kind of broad foundation. Picking where to go deep is what this chapter helps with.

Choosing honestly, not just prestige-chasing

Generative AI and LLMs currently attract the most attention, but attention isn't the same as fit or opportunity. Classical ML and data science roles (Chapters 3–5) remain the largest single category of ML jobs across ordinary companies, not just AI-native ones. Computer vision remains foundational to robotics, manufacturing, and medical imaging. ML infrastructure roles (Chapter 14) are consistently in demand and require real software engineering skill many candidates underestimate. The honest question isn't "which domain is trending" — it's which chapters in this book you found yourself wanting to keep exploring after the required demo was already done.

Part XV · Chapter 15.3

Portfolio Projects Mapped to Every Part

Chapter 15.2 helped narrow down a domain. This chapter is about proving you can actually work in it — not by describing your skills on a resume, but by pointing to something real you built. Below is one concrete project idea for every major Part of this book, plus what actually separates a strong portfolio project from a weak one.

🎻 Frame of reference

No orchestra hires a violinist on the strength of a resume line that says "I can play Bach." They ask for a recording. A portfolio project is the recording — concrete, checkable evidence of exactly the skill a resume can only claim in the abstract.

What separates a strong project from a weak one

The single most common portfolio mistake isn't picking the wrong project — it's picking one too big to finish. An MVP (Minimum Viable Project) is scoped deliberately small enough to actually reach a finished, working, honestly-evaluated end state. A tiny classifier trained on 500 images, properly split into train/val/test (Chapter 5.2), with real metrics (Chapter 5.3) and a documented failure case, is a stronger portfolio piece than an ambitious multi-model system that never got past a half-working notebook.

Four things separate a strong project from a weak one, regardless of domain: it runs end to end, not just in a notebook (ideally with the deployment layer from Part XIV — a model nobody else can run isn't much of a demonstration); it's specific enough to discuss in real depth in an interview, not a copied tutorial; it documents what didn't work as honestly as what did, since real engineering judgment shows up in how failures get handled, not just successes; and it's actually public — a GitHub repository with a working README, not a private folder on a laptop.

Part XV · Chapter 15.4

The Job Search

Chapter 15.2 chose a domain; Chapter 15.3 built evidence for it. This chapter is the pipeline that evidence actually travels through — sourcing, screening, interviewing, and an offer — each stage with its own separate pass criteria, same shape as Chapter 14.8's model pipeline.

🔗 Frame of reference

Chapter 14.8 ran one model through seven stages, each with its own distinct pass criteria — a model that nails experiment tracking still has to separately prove itself at serving, and separately again at monitoring. A job search has the identical shape: a resume that gets past a screen doesn't automatically pass a technical interview, and acing a technical interview doesn't automatically pass a system design round. Optimizing effort for the wrong stage's criteria is the single most common way a real, capable candidate gets stuck.

The stages, and what each one actually tests

A real ML job search runs through a predictable sequence: sourcing (finding postings that actually match Chapter 15.2's chosen domain, not just any posting with "AI" in the title), a resume and portfolio screen (often an ATS (Applicant Tracking System) before any human reads it at all), a technical screen (general coding ability, plus often an ML-specific take-home), a conceptual ML interview, frequently a system design interview for more senior roles, and a behavioral/culture interview — with an offer (and negotiation) only at the very end.
