Part III · Chapter 3.1

Where Data Comes From

Part II built the math and code toolkit assuming a dataset already existed. It doesn't, yet — and where you get it from shapes everything that follows.

🧑‍🍳 Frame of reference

Where does a chef's ingredients come from? Some are grown in a garden out back — cared for personally, exactly what's needed. Some come from the supermarket — pre-packaged, convenient, consistent, but generic. Some come from a farmers market — fresh and specific, but whatever happens to be in season. And some get pulled from behind the restaurant at closing time — free, but you'd better wash it carefully before it goes anywhere near a pot. A dataset is exactly the same: it always comes from somewhere, and that somewhere shapes its quality, cost, and how well it fits what you actually need.

Six common sources

A dataset can come from several genuinely different places, each with its own tradeoffs:

  • Public datasets — pre-built and ready to use (ImageNet, Kaggle, Common Crawl). Fast to start with, but may not match your exact problem.
  • Logs & transactions — data your own product already generates as a byproduct, like purchase history or app usage.
  • Sensors — cameras, microphones, and other devices measuring the physical world directly.
  • Web scraping / APIs — pulled from the internet, often large but messy, and needing real cleanup (Chapter 3.2).
  • Human labeling — people creating data specifically for your task. Expensive, but the closest match to exactly what you need.
  • Synthetic data — artificially generated, for when real examples are too rare, expensive, or sensitive to collect directly.

Synthetic data is worth calling out specifically: it's not "fake" in a dishonest sense — it's a deliberate substitute used when real-world examples of something (a rare accident, a sensitive medical scenario) simply aren't available in enough quantity.

Worked example: detecting potholes

Say a city wants to build a model that spots potholes in road photos. Every source above is actually on the table:

  1. Search for an existing open pothole-detection dataset first — fastest start, but it was photographed in someone else's city, on someone else's roads.
  2. Check the city's own citizen complaint logs — photos already submitted, but inconsistent quality and coverage.
  3. Mount cameras on garbage trucks that already drive every street daily — comprehensive, real sensor data.
  4. Scrape public traffic-camera feeds — free, but messy and raising real privacy questions.
  5. Pay a team to drive around and photograph potholes directly — expensive, but an exact match to the need.
  6. Generate simulated pothole images with a 3D renderer, if real examples of rare pothole types are too scarce.

Part III · Chapter 3.2

Cleaning Messy Data

Chapter 3.1 got the ingredients into your kitchen. Before any of them go in a pot, you wash them, trim the bad parts, and toss what's rotten — that's data cleaning, and it's usually the least glamorous, most time-consuming part of the entire job.

🧼 Frame of reference

You wouldn't cook with unwashed vegetables straight from a delivery box — some need trimming, some are duplicates you didn't mean to order twice, one's labeled wrong, and one looks suspicious enough that you'd want to check it before using it. Raw data arrives the same way: messy, inconsistent, and full of small problems that need attention before anything gets built on top of it.

Four problems that show up constantly

A missing value is a blank entry — nobody recorded it. A duplicate is the same example appearing more than once, which can quietly bias a model toward whatever got repeated. Inconsistent formatting is the same thing written different ways — "NY," "New York," and "new york" as three different-looking values that actually mean one thing. And an outlier is a value far outside the normal range — which might be a genuine rare case, or might just be a typo.

Part III · Chapter 3.3

Exploratory Data Analysis

Chapter 3.2 got your ingredients clean. Before you start cooking, a good chef tastes everything first — a pinch here, a sniff there — to understand what they're actually working with. That's exploratory data analysis.

👃 Frame of reference

You wouldn't start cooking a dish from a new box of ingredients without tasting a few things first — checking how sweet the tomatoes are, how sharp the cheese is, whether anything smells off. Exploratory data analysis (EDA) is exactly that step for a dataset: getting hands-on with it — its statistics, its shape, its relationships — before building anything on top of it.

What EDA actually involves

Exploratory data analysis typically means: checking summary statistics (min, max, average per column), looking at distributions with histograms (Chapters 2.6 and 2.12), and checking relationships between features with scatter plots (Chapters 2.2 and 2.12). The goal isn't to build anything yet — it's to understand what you're holding before you commit to a plan.

Correlation: a number for "how related"

Correlation puts a single number on "how related are these two features," from -1 to +1. It's closely related to the dot product idea from Chapter 2.4, just standardized so it always lands between -1 and 1, regardless of each feature's raw scale.

Worked example: hours studied vs. exam score, revisited

Running EDA on Chapter 1.3's exam-score data confirms what the fitted line already suggested — hours and score move together strongly, a correlation close to +1. But EDA can also surface things a single fitted line hides: maybe one student studied 0 hours yet still scored a 60 — worth a second look, not something the line alone would flag.

Part III · Chapter 3.4

Feature Engineering

Sometimes the most useful number in your dataset isn't one that was ever recorded directly — it's one you build yourself, out of the ones that were.

🏠 Frame of reference

A house listing gives you raw facts: square footage, year built, price. An experienced buyer doesn't just look at those numbers as-is — they compute "price per square foot" to compare listings fairly, or "house age" from the year built, because those derived numbers are often more useful for deciding than the raw facts alone. That's feature engineering: building new, more useful numbers out of the ones you already have.

Combining and extracting

Feature engineering covers a few common moves: combining features (price ÷ square footage = price per square foot), and extracting parts of a feature (pulling "day of week" or "is it a weekend?" out of a raw timestamp — a model can't use "2024-03-15T14:30:00" directly as one meaningful number, but "is it a weekend?" might matter a lot).

Encoding categories as numbers

Chapter 2.2 established that a model needs numbers to work with. But a raw category like "neighborhood: A, B, or C" isn't a number. One-hot encoding fixes this: create one column per category, and put a 1 in whichever column matches, 0 in the rest. "Neighborhood B" becomes the vector [0, 1, 0].

Part III · Chapter 3.5

Imbalanced Data & Why Accuracy Lies

A model can be "99% accurate" and still be completely useless at the one thing it was built to do. This chapter explains exactly how that happens — and it happens constantly.

🏜️ Frame of reference

Imagine a weather forecaster stationed in a desert town where it almost never rains. A lazy forecaster who predicts "no rain" every single day would be right well over 95% of the time — genuinely, technically, "highly accurate." But the rare days it does rain are exactly the days a forecast actually matters, and this forecaster gets every single one of them wrong. High accuracy, zero usefulness.

When one class dominates

Imbalanced data means one class vastly outnumbers another — fraud detection, rare disease screening, manufacturing defect detection all look like this by nature. Accuracy — the percentage of predictions that were correct — sounds like the obvious way to measure a model. On imbalanced data, it's deeply misleading, exactly like the desert forecaster.

Worked example: a lazy fraud detector

A dataset of 1,000 transactions: 10 are fraud (1%), 990 are legitimate. A "model" that predicts "not fraud" for every single transaction, no matter what:

  • Accuracy = 990 / 1,000 = 99%
  • Fraud cases actually caught = 0 out of 10

A genuinely useful model might catch 9 of those 10 fraud cases, but along the way flag a few legitimate transactions by mistake — landing at, say, 96% accuracy. By the single accuracy number, the useless model "wins." This is exactly why other metrics exist (precision and recall, covered fully in Chapter 5.3) that look specifically at performance on the rare class.

Part III · Chapter 3.6

Data Augmentation

What if you can't get more data? Sometimes you don't need to — you can manufacture more training examples out of the ones you already have.

🏀 Frame of reference

A basketball player who only ever practices free throws in a perfectly quiet, perfectly lit gym will struggle the first time they shoot in a loud, unfamiliar arena. A smarter training plan deliberately varies the conditions — different lighting, background noise, fatigue — so the skill generalizes beyond the exact circumstances it was practiced in. A model trained on data has the same weakness: if every example looks the same way in the same conditions, it can fail the moment reality looks even slightly different.

Manufacturing more examples from the ones you have

Data augmentation takes real examples you already have and creates modified copies: flip an image horizontally, rotate it slightly, crop it differently, brighten or darken it. Each version is still unmistakably the same thing — but the model now has to learn to recognize it under more conditions than the one original photo showed. This is different from synthetic data (Chapter 3.1): augmentation modifies real examples you already have; synthetic data invents entirely new examples from a simulator.

Part III · Chapter 3.7 · Deep-dive

Bias in Data, Bias in Models

This closes out Part III by tying its threads together into the single most important warning in the whole Part: a model has no concept of fairness. It only has whatever pattern was in its data.

🪞 Frame of reference

Imagine training a model to recognize "what a great leader looks like," shown only photographs of historical leaders from one narrow demographic. The model would confidently learn that narrow appearance is "what a leader looks like" — not out of malice, and not because it's true, but because that's literally all it ever saw. Chapter 1.3 already established that a model doesn't understand meaning — it fits patterns in data. Bias is the direct, unavoidable consequence of that same fact.

Where bias actually comes from

Bias usually traces back to threads already covered in this Part: who happened to be included during data collection (Chapter 3.1's sources are never neutral), historical decisions baked directly into labels, or imbalanced representation (Chapter 3.5) where one group dominates the training set. None of this requires anyone to act in bad faith — it's a natural, near-inevitable outcome of how data gets collected, which is exactly why it has to be actively looked for rather than assumed away.

Worked example: a resume-screening tool

Imagine a company builds a resume-screening tool trained on ten years of past hiring decisions. If those historical decisions leaned toward hiring one group for a role — for whatever historical reason — the tool learns that lean as "what a good candidate looks like," and can down-rank equally qualified candidates who don't fit the pattern. This isn't hypothetical: several real companies have run into versions of exactly this problem and had to scrap the resulting tools.

  • A skewed training set produces a model with real blind spots on whatever it rarely or never saw.
