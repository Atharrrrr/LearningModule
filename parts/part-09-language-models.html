Part IX · Chapter 9.1

Tokenization

Chapter 8.5's Transformer takes a sequence of vectors as input, adds positional encoding, and runs attention over it. But real text starts as plain characters — "the cat sat" — not vectors. Something has to convert raw text into a sequence of numbers before any of that machinery can run at all. That first, easy to overlook step is tokenization.

🧱 Frame of reference

Imagine a toy factory with two possible strategies. Strategy one: mold a brand-new, one-off custom piece for every single toy design that's ever been requested. Strategy two: keep a modest set of standard interlocking bricks, and build every design — including ones nobody has ever ordered before — by snapping together combinations of those same bricks. The second factory never gets stuck on an order it's never seen; it just reaches for the right combination of pieces it already has. Turning text into model-ready pieces faces the exact same choice.

From text to numbers

Every computation this book has covered — dot products (Chapter 2.4), matrix multiplication (Chapter 2.3), attention (Chapter 8.4) — operates on numbers. Text has to become numbers before any of it applies. Tokenization is that conversion: slice a string into a sequence of tokens, then look each one up in a fixed vocabulary to get an integer ID. Those integer IDs are what actually get turned into vectors — via an embedding table, exactly like Chapter 8.3's word embeddings — before a single Transformer layer ever runs.

Two tempting extremes

The most obvious approach is word-level tokenization: split on spaces and punctuation, and give every distinct word its own ID. It's intuitive, and sequences stay short — one token per word. But a language has an enormous, ever-growing number of distinct words — names, typos, slang, technical terms, words in other languages — and a vocabulary can't realistically contain all of them. Any word missing from the vocabulary is out-of-vocabulary (OOV): the model has no ID for it at all, full stop.

The opposite extreme is character-level tokenization: split into individual characters. Now the vocabulary is tiny — a few dozen letters, digits, and symbols — and OOV essentially disappears, since every word is just a sequence of characters the model already knows. The cost: sequences get 5-10x longer per word, the model has to relearn spelling and word structure from raw characters every time, and Chapter 8.4/8.5's attention has to work over far more positions, which gets expensive fast.

The middle ground: build a vocabulary out of common chunks

Subword tokenization is the middle ground, and the approach virtually every modern language model actually uses. Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) is the classic algorithm for learning one, and its recipe is simple:

  1. Start with a vocabulary of every individual character that appears in the training text.
  2. Count how often every adjacent pair of symbols shows up next to each other, across the entire training text.
  3. Merge the single most frequent pair into one new symbol, and add it to the vocabulary.
  4. Repeat — count, merge, repeat — growing the vocabulary one merged chunk at a time, until it reaches a target size.

Because every merge is chosen from real frequency counts, the chunks that end up in the vocabulary are exactly the pieces that keep recurring across the language — common prefixes, suffixes, and word fragments. A brand-new word can then usually be spelled out of pieces the vocabulary already has, even if the whole word itself was never seen during training.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • Every model in this book operates on numbers — tokenization is the first step that turns raw text into a sequence of integer IDs from a fixed vocabulary.
  • Word-level tokenization is intuitive but breaks on any word missing from its vocabulary (out-of-vocabulary); character-level tokenization avoids that but makes sequences much longer and throws away word structure.
  • Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) learns a subword vocabulary by repeatedly merging whichever adjacent pair of symbols is most frequent in the training text, building up common chunks one merge at a time.
  • Because those chunks are learned from real frequency, an unseen word can usually still be spelled out of pieces the vocabulary already knows — the middle ground between the two extremes.
Quick check
1. What specifically goes wrong with pure word-level tokenization?
Any word missing from its fixed vocabulary (out-of-vocabulary) has no ID at all, and a language has far too many possible words to include them all.
It makes sequences too long to process efficiently.
Word-level tokenization simply doesn't work on any text at all.
2. In Byte-Pair Encoding, what determines which pair of symbols gets merged at each step?
Whichever adjacent pair of symbols currently appears most frequently, counted across the whole training corpus.
Symbols are merged in alphabetical order.
A random pair is picked at each step.
3. Why could the demo tokenize "lowest" correctly, even though "lowest" itself was never in the training corpus?
The learned merges "low" and "est</w>" each came from other training words, and BPE only needs pieces the vocabulary already has, not the exact whole word.
"lowest" was secretly included in the training corpus.
The tokenizer just fell back to raw individual characters.
Part IX · Chapter 9.2

Meet the LLM

Chapter 9.1 turned raw text into a sequence of integer tokens. Chapter 8.5's Transformer can process any sequence of vectors. Put those two pieces together, add one very specific training task, and you get a large language model — the thing behind every chatbot this book's reader has likely already used.

📚 Frame of reference

Imagine an extremely well-read friend playing a party game: you say the first half of a sentence, and they blurt out whatever word feels like the single most natural way to continue it. They're not reading your mind or reasoning about your specific situation — they've simply absorbed so much written language that the most statistically likely next word just occurs to them, almost automatically. That party game, repeated one word at a time until a whole reply exists, is unsettlingly close to literally what a language model does.

One task, trained at enormous scale

A large language model (LLM) is trained on next-token prediction, and nothing else. Feed it a huge amount of real text — a meaningful fraction of the public internet, books, articles, code — and repeatedly ask it: given everything up to this point, what token comes next? Score it with the same cross-entropy loss (Chapter 4.2) used for classification, since predicting "the correct next token out of the whole vocabulary" is really just classification with an enormous number of classes. Run gradient descent (Chapter 4.3) to adjust the Transformer's weights (Chapter 4.1) so its predicted distribution matches the token that actually came next, over and over, across billions of examples. Nothing about the math is new — the scale is the entire story.

Turning next-token prediction into paragraphs

A model that only predicts one next token isn't obviously useful on its own. Autoregressive generation is the trick that turns it into paragraphs: predict a distribution over the next token, pick one, glue it onto the sequence, and feed the whole thing back in as input for predicting the token after that. Greedy decoding always takes the single most likely token; sampling instead rolls a weighted die using the model's own probabilities. The demo below makes the difference between them concrete.

✓ What you can now explain to someone else
  • A large language model is a Transformer trained on one task: next-token prediction — given every token so far, output a probability distribution over what token comes next.
  • "Large" refers to both the volume of training text and the number of parameters in the network, trained with the same cross-entropy loss and gradient descent used since Chapter 4.
  • Autoregressive generation turns single-token predictions into paragraphs: predict, pick one, append it, feed the longer sequence back in, repeat — each new token conditioned on everything before it, including tokens the model itself just generated.
  • Greedy decoding (always the top choice) can trap generation in repetitive loops; sampling (weighted randomness) is one way to avoid that, at the cost of some predictability.
Quick check
1. What single task is a large language model actually trained to perform?
Given a sequence of tokens, predict a probability distribution over what the next token should be.
Directly verifying whether a statement is factually true.
Sorting training examples into a fixed set of labeled categories.
2. In the demo, why does clicking "Greedy" repeatedly from "the" eventually loop forever on "the cat sat on the cat sat on..."?
Greedy decoding always picks the exact same highest-probability word for a given current word, so once the sequence revisits that word, it's forced to retrace the identical path again.
The training data itself changes with every click.
This only happens because of a bug in the demo, not because of how greedy decoding actually works.
3. What does "autoregressive" mean in autoregressive generation?
The model's own previously generated tokens get fed back in as part of the input for predicting the next one, over and over.
The model repeatedly rewrites tokens it already generated earlier.
All output tokens are produced simultaneously, in a single step.
