| cross-validation | 5.2 Train/Val/Test & Cross-Validation | A technique that splits the training data into k folds, rotating which fold is held out as validation each round, training k times and averaging the results — a more robust estimate than a single validation split, especially useful with limited data. |

| L1/L2 regularization | 5.4 Regularization | Two common penalty shapes: L1 penalizes the sum of the absolute values of the weights and tends to push unhelpful weights all the way to zero. | backpropagation | 6.4 Backpropagation | The algorithm that computes the loss's gradient with respect to every weight in a neural network, by applying the chain rule repeatedly, working backward from the output layer to the input layer one layer at a time. |

| momentum | 6.5 Optimizers (SGD, Adam) | An optimizer that accumulates a running velocity from past gradients instead of reacting only to the current one — like a ball rolling downhill that keeps some of its speed, smoothing out zigzags and powering through small flat patches. |

| epoch | 6.6 The Training Toolbox | One complete pass through the entire training dataset. A model is typically trained for many epochs, seeing every training example repeatedly. |
| learning rate schedule | 6.6 The Training Toolbox | A plan for changing the learning rate over the course of training, instead of holding it fixed — typically starting higher, for fast early progress, and decreasing over time for careful, stable convergence. |
| vanishing gradient | 6.7 Vanishing Gradients & Initialization | A gradient that shrinks toward zero as it's propagated backward through many layers, because each layer's local derivative is a fraction less than 1, and those fractions multiply together. | weight initialization | 6.7 Vanishing Gradients & Initialization | The choice of starting values for a network's weights before training begins. | out-of-vocabulary (OOV) | 9.1 Tokenization | A word encountered at use time that never appeared in training and has no entry in the vocabulary — a tokenizer with no fallback simply has no way to represent it. |
| subword tokenization | 9.1 Tokenization | Splitting text into pieces larger than a single character but smaller than a whole word — common prefixes, suffixes, and word fragments — learned from how often they actually occur in real text. |
| Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) | 9.1 Tokenization | An algorithm for learning a subword vocabulary: start from individual characters, then repeatedly merge whichever adjacent pair of symbols is currently most frequent across the training text, adding each merged result as a new vocabulary entry. |
| large language model (LLM) | 9.2 Meet the LLM | A Transformer (Chapter 8.5) trained on a huge amount of text to do one specific thing: given a sequence of tokens, predict a probability distribution over what the very next token should be. "Large" refers to both the volume of training text and the number of parameters (Chapter 1.3) in the network, often in the billions. |
| next-token prediction | 9.2 Meet the LLM | The training task an LLM is built around: given every token that came before it, output a probability distribution (Chapter 8.4's softmax) over the entire vocabulary (Chapter 9.1) for what token comes next. |
| autoregressive generation | 9.2 Meet the LLM | Generating text one token at a time: feed in the current sequence, get back a probability distribution for the next token, pick one, append it to the sequence, then feed that longer sequence back in and repeat. Each new token is generated using every token before it — including tokens the model itself just produced. |
| greedy decoding | 9.2 Meet the LLM | A generation strategy that always picks the single highest-probability token at every step. Simple and deterministic, but a locally "best" choice at every step doesn't guarantee a good result overall — it can trap generation in a repeating loop. |
| sampling | 9.2 Meet the LLM | A generation strategy that picks the next token randomly, weighted by the model's own predicted probabilities, instead of always taking the single most likely one. Higher-probability tokens still get picked more often, but the randomness can break out of the repetitive loops greedy decoding falls into. |
| scaling law | 9.3 Scaling Laws & Emergent Abilities | An empirical finding that a language model's next-token-prediction loss decreases smoothly and predictably as you increase model size (parameters), training data size, or compute — following a power-law-shaped curve across many orders of magnitude, with no sign of leveling off until data or compute actually runs short. |
| emergent ability | 9.3 Scaling Laws & Emergent Abilities | A capability that appears to jump from near-zero to strong performance once model scale crosses some threshold, rather than improving gradually alongside scale — for example, a smaller model failing almost completely at a multi-step task while a larger one suddenly succeeds. |
| base model | 9.4 Instruction Tuning & RLHF | An LLM trained only on next-token prediction (Chapter 9.2) over raw text, with no additional training to make it behave like a helpful assistant. Left alone, it simply continues whatever pattern of text it's given — which may or may not mean answering a question helpfully. |
| instruction tuning (supervised fine-tuning, SFT) | 9.4 Instruction Tuning & RLHF | Continuing to train a base model — using the exact same next-token-prediction loss and gradient descent (Chapters 4.3, 6.4-6.5) as before — on a curated dataset of (prompt, ideal response) pairs written or vetted by humans, instead of raw internet text. This nudges the model's default completion style toward directly answering instructions. |
| reward model | 9.4 Instruction Tuning & RLHF | A separate, smaller model trained to predict which of two candidate responses to the same prompt a human would prefer. Trained on many human comparisons ("response A is better than response B"), it outputs a single score — higher for responses closer to what people actually want. |
| RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) | 9.4 Instruction Tuning & RLHF | A training stage that uses a reward model's scores as the reward signal (Chapter 1.1's reinforcement learning) for further training the LLM itself — typically with an RL algorithm called PPO. The LLM generates a response, the reward model scores it, and the LLM's weights are nudged to make higher-scoring responses more likely next time. |
| low-rank | 9.5 Efficient Fine-Tuning (LoRA, quantization) | A matrix that can be reconstructed, with little or no loss, as the product of two much "thinner" matrices — meaning it holds far less independent information than its full size suggests, the same underlying idea behind Chapter 4.9's dimensionality reduction, now applied to a weight matrix (Chapter 2.3) instead of a dataset. |
| LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) | 9.5 Efficient Fine-Tuning (LoRA, quantization) | A fine-tuning method that freezes a pretrained model's original weight matrices completely, and instead trains a much smaller pair of matrices whose product forms an additive low-rank update on top of them. Far fewer parameters need gradients or optimizer state, while the frozen original weights preserve everything the base model already learned. |
| quantization | 9.5 Efficient Fine-Tuning (LoRA, quantization) | Storing a model's weights using fewer bits per number — 8-bit or 4-bit integers instead of the usual 16-bit or 32-bit floating point — shrinking memory footprint and speeding up computation, at the cost of some numeric precision. |
| prompt | 9.6 Prompting & In-Context Learning | The text input given to an LLM — instructions, context, examples, or all three — that the model conditions its next-token predictions (Chapter 9.2) on. Changing the prompt changes the output without changing the model's weights at all. |
| zero-shot | 9.6 Prompting & In-Context Learning | Asking a model to perform a task by describing it directly in the prompt, with no worked examples included. |
| few-shot prompting | 9.6 Prompting & In-Context Learning | Including a handful of worked examples directly in the prompt before asking the model to handle a new case — as opposed to zero-shot, which only describes the task in words. |
| in-context learning | 9.6 Prompting & In-Context Learning | An LLM's ability to infer a task or pattern purely from a few examples shown in the prompt, and apply it to a new input — without any weights being updated at all. The "learning" happens entirely within one prompt's context, not through training. |
| RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) | 9.7 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) | A technique that, before generating a response, first retrieves the most relevant chunks of text from an external document collection and inserts them into the prompt (Chapter 9.6) as extra context — letting a frozen LLM answer using specific, current, or private information it was never trained on. |
| retrieval | 9.7 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) | Finding the most relevant document chunks for a given query out of a large collection, typically by comparing embeddings (Chapter 8.3) with a similarity measure like cosine similarity (Chapter 2.4's dot product, normalized by vector length) and keeping the closest matches. |
| agent | 9.8 Agents & Tool Use | An LLM used in a loop where, instead of just answering directly, it can choose to call an external tool, wait for that tool's real result, and decide what to do next — repeating this cycle until it has enough information to give a final answer. |
| tool use (function calling) | 9.8 Agents & Tool Use | Describing available external tools (a calculator, a search engine, a code interpreter) in the prompt (Chapter 9.6), and having the LLM output a structured request to call one with specific arguments. The tool actually runs outside the model; its real output gets inserted back into the prompt as new context before the model continues. |
| hallucination | 9.9 Hallucination & Evaluation | An LLM producing fluent, confident-sounding text that is factually false or entirely made up, with no internal signal distinguishing it from a true statement. A direct consequence of next-token prediction (Chapter 9.2) rewarding plausible continuations, not verified truth. |
| benchmark | 9.9 Hallucination & Evaluation | A standardized, fixed set of test questions or tasks with known correct answers, used to measure and compare LLMs' capabilities with a single number — Chapter 5.3's accuracy idea, applied to language models. |
| VLM (vision-language model) | 10.1 Meet the VLM (CLIP) | A model that processes both images and text and represents them in a shared embedding space (Chapter 8.3), so that an image and a text description of that same thing end up with similar embeddings — even though the two started as completely different kinds of input. |
| CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) | 10.1 Meet the VLM (CLIP) | A VLM trained on a huge number of (image, caption) pairs scraped from the internet, using a contrastive loss: for a batch of pairs, pull each image's embedding close to its own caption's embedding, and push it away from every other caption's embedding in the same batch. |
| contrastive learning | 10.1 Meet the VLM (CLIP) | A training approach that doesn't require exact correct-answer labels for every input — instead, for a batch of matched pairs, it pulls each true pair's embeddings close together (Chapter 2.4's cosine similarity, maximized) and pushes every mismatched pair apart, learning structure purely from which items belong together. |
| image captioning | 10.2 Captioning & Visual Question Answering | Generating a natural-language description of an image's content by feeding the image's features (Chapter 10.1) into a text generator as context, then producing tokens autoregressively (Chapter 9.2) exactly like ordinary text generation — just conditioned on an image instead of a text prompt. |
| visual question answering (VQA) | 10.2 Captioning & Visual Question Answering | Answering a specific text question about a specific image, by conditioning generation (Chapter 9.2) on both the image's features and the question's tokens together — producing a targeted answer instead of an unprompted, general description. |
| waveform | 10.3 Speech & Audio Models (Whisper) | A sequence of numbers representing air pressure measured at a fixed rate many times per second (the sample rate) — the raw numeric form audio takes before any further processing, directly analogous to Chapter 2.11's arrays. |
| spectrogram | 10.3 Speech & Audio Models (Whisper) | A 2D grid built from a waveform, with time along one axis and frequency along the other, where each cell's value says how strongly that frequency was present at that moment. Converts a 1D waveform into an image-like grid (Chapter 7.1) that a CNN or Transformer can process with the same machinery used for actual images. |
| Whisper | 10.3 Speech & Audio Models (Whisper) | A speech-to-text model built as a Transformer encoder-decoder (Chapter 8.5): the encoder processes a spectrogram of the input audio, and the decoder autoregressively generates the transcript's text tokens (Chapters 9.1, 9.2), conditioned on the encoded audio — the same shape as Chapter 10.2's image captioning, with a spectrogram standing in for an image. |
| autoencoder | 11.1 Autoencoders | A neural network trained to reconstruct its own input, split into an encoder that compresses the input down to a much smaller vector, and a decoder that reconstructs the original input from that small vector alone. Trained by minimizing reconstruction error (Chapter 4.3) between the input and the decoder's output. |
| latent representation (bottleneck) | 11.1 Autoencoders | The small, compressed vector at an autoencoder's narrowest point, sitting between the encoder and decoder. Forced to be much smaller than the input, it can only capture the input's essential structure — whatever's needed to reconstruct it reasonably well. |
| variational autoencoder (VAE) | 11.2 Variational Autoencoders | An autoencoder whose encoder outputs the parameters of a probability distribution over latent codes — typically the mean and standard deviation of a normal distribution (Chapter 2.6) — instead of one fixed point. Trained so that samples drawn from that distribution decode well, and so the overall latent space stays organized enough that a random sample from anywhere reasonable in it decodes to something plausible. |
| generative adversarial network (GAN) | 11.3 GANs | Two networks trained against each other: a generator that produces fake data from random noise, and a discriminator that tries to distinguish real training data from the generator's fakes. Each network's training signal comes directly from the other's current performance, not from a fixed reconstruction target. |
| generator | 11.3 GANs | The network in a GAN that takes random noise as input and outputs fake data, trained to make that fake data score as "real" as possible according to the current discriminator. |
| discriminator | 11.3 GANs | The network in a GAN trained as an ordinary classifier (Chapter 4.2): given an input, output the probability it's real training data rather than something the generator produced. |
| forward diffusion process | 11.4 Diffusion Models | A fixed, mechanical procedure that gradually adds a small amount of Gaussian noise (Chapter 2.6) to real data over many steps, until after enough steps the result is indistinguishable from pure random noise. Requires no training at all — just a known noise-adding schedule, applied step by step. |
| reverse diffusion process (denoising) | 11.4 Diffusion Models | A neural network trained to look at a noisy sample at some step and predict the noise that was added to reach it, using ordinary supervised learning (Chapter 4.3) — since the forward process's real noise at each step is always known during training, providing a genuine target. Generation then runs this trained denoiser backward, starting from pure random noise and removing a small amount at a time, until a coherent new sample emerges. |
| conditioning | 11.5 Text-to-Image (Diffusion Meets CLIP) | Feeding extra information — here, a text prompt's CLIP embedding (Chapter 10.1) — into a generative model's every step, so its output is steered toward matching that information, not left to generate something generic. |
| environment | 12.1 Learning by Trial and Reward | Everything a reinforcement learning agent interacts with — whatever it takes actions in and receives feedback from. Could be a game, a simulated robot arm, a website, or anything else with a defined set of states, actions, and rewards. |
| state | 12.1 Learning by Trial and Reward | A snapshot of the environment at a given moment — everything the agent currently knows or observes about its situation, used to decide what action to take next. |
| action | 12.1 Learning by Trial and Reward | A choice the agent makes at a given state — a move, a decision, a response — which changes the environment and determines what reward and next state follow. |
| reward | 12.1 Learning by Trial and Reward | A number the environment gives back after an action, saying how good or bad that action turned out to be right now. The agent never receives a correct-answer label (Chapter 1.2) — only this ongoing stream of reward numbers. |
| policy | 12.1 Learning by Trial and Reward | The agent's strategy for choosing an action given a state — whatever rule or function currently determines its behavior. Reinforcement learning's whole goal is improving the policy so it earns more total reward over time. |
| Q-value | 12.2 Q-Learning, Simply Explained | The expected total future reward of taking a specific action in a specific state, and acting well from then on. Written Q(s, a) — one number for every state-action pair, capturing not just the immediate reward but everything that action leads to afterward. |
| Q-learning | 12.2 Q-Learning, Simply Explained | An algorithm that learns Q-values purely from experience: after taking action a in state s, observing reward r and landing in state s', nudge Q(s, a) toward r plus the best Q-value available from s' — bootstrapping off its own current estimate of the future, the same nudge-toward-a-target idea as gradient descent (Chapter 4.3), applied to a table of values instead of model weights. |
| policy gradient | 12.3 Policy Gradients & Exploration/Exploitation | A reinforcement learning method that directly adjusts a policy's action probabilities — typically via softmax (Chapter 8.4) over learnable scores — instead of learning Q-values first and picking the best one. After an action's real outcome is observed, its probability is nudged up if the outcome was better than expected, and down if worse, via gradient ascent. |
| exploitation | 12.3 Policy Gradients & Exploration/Exploitation | Choosing whatever action currently looks best, based on estimates learned so far, to maximize immediate expected reward. |
| exploration | 12.3 Policy Gradients & Exploration/Exploitation | Deliberately trying an action that doesn't currently look best, specifically to learn more about it — at the cost of possibly earning less reward right now. |
| deep reinforcement learning (deep RL) | 12.4 Deep RL & RLHF Revisited | Using a neural network (Chapter 6) instead of a table to represent Q-values (Chapter 12.2) or a policy (Chapter 12.3), when the number of possible states is too large — or continuous — to ever enumerate one table entry per state. The network generalizes across similar states instead of memorizing one value per exact state, the same generalization ability that made CNNs (Chapter 7.2) and Transformers (Chapter 8.5) useful in the first place. |
| demographic parity | 13.1 Fairness & Bias | A fairness check comparing the positive-prediction rate across groups: what fraction of each group gets a positive prediction, regardless of whether that prediction is correct. A gap here means one group is flagged positive more often than another. |
| equal opportunity | 13.1 Fairness & Bias | A fairness check comparing recall (Chapter 5.3's true positive rate) across groups: among people who are genuinely positive, what fraction does the model correctly identify in each group? A gap here means the model misses genuinely qualified people in one group far more often than in another. |
| memorization | 13.2 Privacy, Safety & Misuse | A model reproducing a specific training example nearly verbatim, rather than a generalized pattern learned across many examples — most likely for rare or unique strings that appeared only once or twice, since there was little repeated pattern available to compress them into a general rule. |
| jailbreak (adversarial prompt) | 13.2 Privacy, Safety & Misuse | A carefully crafted input designed to bypass a model's trained safety behavior (Chapter 9.4's RLHF), getting it to produce output it was specifically trained to refuse. |
| interpretability | 13.3 Interpretability | The ability to understand and explain why a model made one specific prediction, not just whether it's accurate overall (Chapter 5.3). Some model types make this easy and exact; others make it genuinely difficult. |
| black box | 13.3 Interpretability | A model complex enough — millions of interacting weights across many nonlinear layers (Chapters 6-8) — that no small set of numbers can exactly reconstruct why it produced one specific output, the way a linear model's feature contributions can. |
| model checkpoint | 14.1 Version Control for ML | A saved snapshot of a model's parameters (weights) at some point during or after training — typically a large binary file, often hundreds of megabytes to many gigabytes for real models. |
| hashing (for large files) | 14.1 Version Control for ML | Computing a short, fixed-length fingerprint from a large file's exact contents. Two identical files always produce the same hash; any change at all produces a completely different one. Tools built for ML version control store the large file itself separately, and let the lightweight code repository track just its hash — enough to know exactly which version of a huge file belongs with which commit, without storing full copies inside git itself. |
| reproducibility | 14.1 Version Control for ML | The ability to recreate the exact same trained model again later — which requires tracking not just the code, but the exact data version, hyperparameters, and random seed that produced it, since any of those alone can change the result. |
| hyperparameter | 14.2 Experiment Tracking | A setting chosen before training begins — like a learning rate (Chapter 6.5) or batch size (Chapter 6.6) — as opposed to a parameter (Chapter 1.3), which is learned from data during training itself. |
| experiment tracking | 14.2 Experiment Tracking | Systematically logging every training run's hyperparameters, resulting metrics, and a pointer to the resulting model checkpoint (Chapter 14.1), so different runs can be compared and the best one identified and reproduced later. |
| parallelization | 14.3 Hardware: CPU vs. GPU vs. TPU | Doing many independent pieces of a computation at the same time, rather than one after another — possible whenever those pieces don't depend on each other's results. |
| GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) | 14.3 Hardware: CPU vs. GPU vs. TPU | Hardware with thousands of simple compute cores, originally built for rendering graphics — coloring millions of independent pixels, another massively parallel job — and now widely repurposed for neural network training and inference, since matrix multiplication has the exact same "many independent simple operations" shape. |
| TPU (Tensor Processing Unit) | 14.3 Hardware: CPU vs. GPU vs. TPU | Hardware built specifically for neural network tensor (Chapter 2.11) operations, going even further than a GPU's general-purpose parallel cores — trading away flexibility for other kinds of computation in exchange for even greater speed at exactly the matrix-multiply-shaped work neural networks need. |
| data parallelism | 14.4 Distributed & Parallel Training | Splitting a training batch (Chapter 6.6) into smaller chunks, sending each chunk to a different GPU running an identical copy of the current model, having each GPU compute gradients (Chapter 6.4) on its own chunk, then averaging all the GPUs' gradients together before applying one optimizer update (Chapter 6.5). |
| model parallelism | 14.4 Distributed & Parallel Training | Splitting a single model's layers across multiple GPUs or machines, used when the model itself is too large to fit in any one GPU's memory — data flows through the layers on one device, then the next device, in sequence, trading added communication between devices for the memory capacity to hold the model at all. |
| inference | 14.5 Serving a Model | Using an already-trained model to produce a prediction on a new, live input — as opposed to training (Chapter 4.3), the earlier process that produced the model's weights in the first place. |
| latency | 14.5 Serving a Model | The time between a single request arriving and its response being returned — what an individual user actually experiences waiting for an answer. |
| throughput | 14.5 Serving a Model | The total number of requests a system can process per unit of time overall — what matters for serving many users at once, as opposed to any single user's individual wait. |
| container | 14.6 Containers & Reproducibility | A package bundling code together with its exact software dependencies — library versions, system tools, configuration — into one portable, self-contained unit that runs identically regardless of the underlying machine it's deployed on. |
| pinning (dependency versions) | 14.6 Containers & Reproducibility | Specifying an exact, fixed version of every software dependency a project relies on, instead of "whatever the latest version happens to be" — guaranteeing the same library behavior every time the project runs, anywhere. |
| data drift | 14.7 Monitoring & Drift | The statistical distribution of live, real-world input data gradually shifting away from the distribution of the data a model was trained on, breaking the Chapter 5.1/5.2 assumption that training data and future data look statistically alike. |
| monitoring | 14.7 Monitoring & Drift | Continuously tracking statistics about a deployed model's live inputs and predictions after deployment, specifically to catch data drift or other problems early — since production typically lacks the immediate ground-truth labels available during training. |
| ML pipeline | 14.8 A Real Pipeline, Start to Finish | The full ordered sequence of stages a model passes through on the way from a trained checkpoint to a running, monitored product — versioning, experiment tracking, hardware-aware training, scaling, serving, packaging, and monitoring — each stage feeding the next. |
| abstract | 15.1 How to Read a Research Paper | A short paragraph at the very start of a paper, written by its own authors, summarizing the problem, the approach, and the headline result in a few sentences — the paper's own answer to "what is this and why should I keep reading?" |
| ablation study | 15.1 How to Read a Research Paper | An experiment where researchers deliberately remove or disable one piece of their own method, then re-measure performance — isolating exactly how much that one piece actually contributed, rather than just reporting the full system's final score. |
| T-shaped skill set | 15.2 Choosing a Specialization | A working shape of expertise: broad, working knowledge across an entire field (the horizontal bar of the T) combined with deep, specialized expertise in one particular area of it (the vertical stroke) — as opposed to being equally shallow everywhere, or narrow everywhere with no broader context. |
| MVP (Minimum Viable Project) | 15.3 Portfolio Projects Mapped to Every Part | A project deliberately scoped down to something small enough to actually finish, end to end, rather than an ambitious project left half-built. A complete small thing demonstrates far more than an incomplete large one. |
| ATS (Applicant Tracking System) | 15.4 The Job Search | Software that scans and ranks incoming resumes/applications before a human ever reads most of them, typically by matching keywords against the job posting — meaning a resume genuinely needs the posting's specific terms present, not just a synonym or an implied skill. |
| system design interview | 15.4 The Job Search | An interview format asking a candidate to design a full system for an open-ended problem — for an ML role, often something like "design a pipeline for X" (Chapter 14.8's shape) — evaluating structured thinking and tradeoff awareness over one exact correct answer. |
